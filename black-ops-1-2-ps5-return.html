Black Ops and Black Ops 2 Are Finally Coming Back to PlayStation, and It Is About Time

Treyarch confirmed both games are landing on PlayStation this July with Campaign, Multiplayer, and Zombies intact. It closes a gap that should never have existed in the first place, and not everyone is thrilled about where Activision's attention went instead.

June 20, 2026 by Respawnerz Staff 09 min read

PlayStation players have been locked out of two of Call of Duty's best games for the better part of a decade, and not by choice. Black Ops and Black Ops 2 never got the backward compatibility treatment Xbox gave its own back catalog after the PS3 generation ended. If you wanted to replay Kino der Toten or run it back on Nuketown, your options were a dusty PS3, a used console hunt, or a streaming service that depended on how good your internet was that night.

That ends in July. Treyarch confirmed both games are coming to PlayStation with Campaign, Multiplayer, and Zombies all included, no asterisks attached. It sounds like a routine re-release. It is not, and the reasons why say more about Activision's priorities, and about a growing fight over what "backward compatibility" is supposed to mean in 2026, than the announcement itself lets on.

A Decade-Long Gap That Never Made Sense

Sony's approach to legacy access has always been a patchwork. PS Plus Premium streams a rotating slice of older titles, and the quality of that experience lives and dies by your connection. Microsoft built an actual backward compatibility program. Sony never did, and Call of Duty's two most beloved entries from the PS3 era paid the price.

That's not a small loss. Black Ops gave the series one of its sharpest campaigns, full of Cold War paranoia and a twist that's still referenced in forums a decade and a half later. Black Ops 2 pushed further with branching story paths and a multiplayer build that a lot of competitive players still rank above anything the franchise has shipped since.

And then there's Zombies. Kino der Toten, Five, Ascension, Mob of the Dead, Origins, these maps built actual communities. Easter egg hunters spent years cracking them open on YouTube. Speedrunners turned them into a subgenre with its own leaderboard culture, its own slang, its own arguments about route optimization that still play out in comment sections a decade later. Groups of friends who haven't touched a controller together in years still bring up that one Mob of the Dead run that went sideways. Locking that experience behind a PS3 nobody kept plugged in was always going to be a problem eventually.

It's worth sitting with how unusual this gap actually was. Call of Duty is not a niche franchise digging through its back catalog for completionists. It's the best-selling shooter series in the industry, and two of its most influential entries were functionally unavailable to a huge chunk of its own player base for the better part of a decade. Compare that to how the industry has handled other major franchise back catalogs in recent years, with remasters, ports, and subscription bundles arriving within a few years of a console transition rather than ten. Call of Duty's own earlier titles, the ones built before the Black Ops sub-series even existed, mostly made that jump already. Black Ops and Black Ops 2 sat out the entire conversation.

Part of that comes down to how messy PS3-to-PS4 backward compatibility was at a technical level. Sony's architecture shift between those two generations was a bigger leap than Microsoft's, and that made native compatibility a harder engineering problem rather than a simple licensing decision. That explanation has been around for years, and most players have heard it. It just stopped being a satisfying answer somewhere around the fifth straight generation of asking for these games back.

Why Now, and Not Five Years Ago

The math on this one isn't complicated. Building a new AAA shooter from scratch takes years and costs more every cycle. Porting a thirteen-year-old game with an established codebase is cheap by comparison, and Activision knows exactly who's buying it.

The players who grew up on Xbox 360 and PS3-era Call of Duty are now in their late twenties and thirties, with steady jobs and the kind of disposable income a teenager playing Black Ops 2 after school never had. Nostalgia sells, and few Call of Duty eras carry as much weight as this one. You don't need a marketing study to confirm that. Just check how often "Mob of the Dead" or "Nuketown" trends whenever the franchise drops a throwback skin.

There's a scheduling angle too. Major releases keep sliding, and Activision needs something to keep the brand visible in the gaps between them. A July launch lands in a notoriously quiet stretch of the release calendar, right when publishers are hunting for anything that can hold attention before the back half of the year gets crowded. These ports don't need blockbuster numbers to justify themselves. They just need to remind people why this era of Call of Duty is still talked about, and based on the response since the announcement, that part is already working.

It also doesn't hurt that Activision gets to frame this as a win for players right after a stretch where the franchise has taken its fair share of criticism over monetization and yearly release fatigue. A goodwill play that costs relatively little to produce and generates almost entirely positive headlines is an easy call from a publisher's seat, even if the cynicism behind the timing is obvious to anyone paying attention.

Port, Not Remaster, and That Distinction Matters

Here's where the optimism gets tempered. These are ports. Not remasters. Treyarch hasn't promised rebuilt visuals, modernized animations, or new anti-cheat infrastructure, and that's a real gap from what some fans were hoping for after years of waiting.

It's not automatically bad news. Plenty of players want the original experience preserved exactly as it was, cracked menu music and all. There's a real argument that a faithful port respects the games more than a reworked remaster would, especially for a community that's spent years picking apart exactly what made the original Zombies maps feel the way they did. Change the pacing, change the gunplay feel even slightly, and you risk breaking something that wasn't actually broken.

But faithful preservation only works if the infrastructure underneath it actually holds up. That leaves real questions sitting unanswered. Will the multiplayer servers get any kind of upgrade, or are players walking into the same connection issues that plagued the originals near the end of their PS3 lifespan? Will all the DLC maps make the cut, including the Zombies content that was sold separately across multiple season passes back when Black Ops 2 was still active? Is cheating going to be a problem on day one, the way it has been for nearly every older Call of Duty re-release in recent memory, including ones that launched with anti-cheat systems specifically built for the relaunch?

There's also the matchmaking question, which sounds minor until you actually try to populate a thirteen-year-old game's player base from scratch on a new platform. Cross-play between Xbox's existing backward-compatible version and a brand-new PlayStation release isn't guaranteed just because both platforms technically run the same game. If Activision splits the player base along platform lines instead of unifying it, the early weeks could feel a lot emptier than the announcement trailer suggested.

Activision hasn't addressed any of it directly. Until they do, the excitement around this announcement is going to keep sharing space with a healthy amount of caution. Fans want the games back. They don't want every old problem coming back with them.

The Pushback Is Coming From an Unexpected Place

You'd expect the loudest complaints to come from PlayStation players who feel like they're getting a watered-down product. Instead, a chunk of the frustration is coming from Xbox players, who've had access to both games through backward compatibility for years already.

The issue isn't that PlayStation is getting the games. It's what the decision signals about where Microsoft is putting its resources since acquiring Activision Blizzard. A segment of the Xbox community expected that acquisition to mean real investment in their existing back catalog: anti-cheat overhauls, Game Pass integration for classic titles, maybe even meaningful updates to games they already own. Watching that engineering time go toward a PlayStation port instead has reopened an old argument about what platform exclusivity is even worth anymore in a Microsoft-owned Call of Duty.

From Microsoft's side, the logic barely needs explaining. More available platforms means more buyers, and more buyers means a better return on an acquisition that cost the better part of seventy billion dollars. But for the players who stuck with Xbox specifically because of moments like this, the announcement reads less like good news for the franchise and more like confirmation that brand loyalty doesn't buy you much anymore.

That frustration has shown up across the usual places, Reddit threads, X replies under the official announcement, comment sections on gaming forums that have been tracking Call of Duty's backward compatibility gaps since well before the Microsoft deal closed. The common thread isn't outrage at PlayStation players getting access. It's a sense that the acquisition was supposed to change something tangible about how these older titles get treated, and so far the most visible change has been expanding their reach to a competitor's platform instead of improving the version Xbox players already own. Whether that reaction holds up once more details about the actual technical investment in this port come out remains to be seen. Right now, it's the dominant tone in a lot of the discussion.

What a Strong Launch Could Unlock

If these ports land well in July, Activision gets a clear signal: there's still real demand for the older Call of Duty catalog, and it isn't being satisfied by streaming workarounds or hardware nobody wants to keep around. World at War, Modern Warfare 2, Modern Warfare 3, even Black Ops 3, all of it becomes a more realistic next step if this one performs.

That's the part worth watching past launch week. Game preservation in this industry has been inconsistent for years, with entire console generations sitting just out of reach while publishers chase the next release. Whether this is the start of Activision actually fixing that, or just a one-off nostalgia play timed to a quiet stretch on the release calendar, comes down to how July goes.

Either way, Black Ops and Black Ops 2 are getting another shot at the audience that's been asking for them since the PS3 generation ended. That part, at least, is overdue.
