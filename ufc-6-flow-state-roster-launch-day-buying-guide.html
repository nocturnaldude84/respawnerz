UFC 6's Launch Week Problem: Great Reviews, Broken Servers, and a Roster Down 60 Fighters

EA Sports UFC 6 picked up the best review scores in franchise history, then spent its first day issuing a community update about crashes EA had already confirmed. Here is what the new Flow State system actually does, why the roster shrank instead of grew, and whether any of this should change what you do this weekend.

June 20, 2026 by Respawnerz Staff 11 min read

EA Sports UFC 6 launched on June 19 with the best review scores the franchise has ever gotten. An 84 on Metacritic. An 82 average on OpenCritic. GameSpew called it the most entertaining and authentic UFC game yet, and PlayStation Universe went with "championship material."

Less than a day later, EA was already publishing a community update admitting to online crashes, matchmaking failures, and a new combat system that a fair chunk of the reviewing press doesn't like. Scroll through the EA Forums and you'll find a thread of white-screen crashes mid-match, PS5 consoles overheating just sitting in the menu screen, and ranked losses handed to players whose only mistake was losing connection at the wrong moment.

This isn't really a story about whether UFC 6 is good. By most accounts it's the best entry the series has had. It's a story about what "launch day" actually means for a sports game in 2026, and there are three separate threads worth pulling on here: the new system everyone's arguing about, the roster that somehow got smaller, and whether you should have bought any of this on day one.

Flow State, explained

The headline new feature is something EA is calling Flow State, a three-layer momentum system riding on top of Frostbite's new ragdoll physics. It's EA Vancouver's first real attempt at full real-time physics simulation for this series.

Here's how it works. Every fighter gets five passive perks and one activated ability, drawn from a pool of 30 total options, and the picks are supposed to match how that fighter actually fights in real life. A pressure boxer builds momentum one way. A counter-striker builds it another way. The goal is that playing as a grappler should feel mechanically different from playing as a striker, not just look different in the cutscenes.

The Flow Meter fills based on how well you're matching that fighter's real tendencies. Throw the kind of strikes a pressure fighter throws, and it climbs faster than if you're improvising outside the archetype. Once it peaks, you get a payoff window where that fighter's signature tools get amplified for a few seconds.

It's solving a real problem. Older UFC games let you play any fighter the exact same way as long as your inputs were clean, and Flow State is the first serious attempt to make fighter identity actually matter on the stick, not just in the character select screen.

Take a wrestling-heavy grinder versus a pure striker as an example. In UFC 5, both could win the same way: land strikes, manage stamina, finish with the same string of inputs regardless of who you picked. In UFC 6, the grappler's Flow Meter is built to climb off takedown attempts and top control, while the striker's climbs off clean counters and combination volume. Play the grappler like a kickboxer and the meter barely moves. That's the system doing what it's supposed to do. It's also exactly the kind of thing that feels great in a single-player career run and unpredictable in a ranked match where your opponent doesn't care about your build, they just want to win.

Whether it lands is where reviewers split, and not by a little. Newsweek called it "the game's one big problem," arguing the activated state breaks the flow of a competitive match and feels more like a random buff than earned skill. GameSpew scored the game a 90 and named Flow State as the reason UFC 6 finally feels authentic. COGconnected sat in the middle, praising the ambition while admitting the visual flash when a fighter activates Flow State is jarring enough to throw off your read on the fight.

This isn't really a disagreement about whether the system is well built. It's a disagreement about what people want a fighting game to be. If you want every match to be a clean test of mechanical skill, an extra layer of fighter-specific buffs is going to feel like noise. If you want a fighter to actually feel like that fighter, Flow State is the first real swing the series has taken at it. Both are reasonable ways to judge a game, which is exactly why the same system shows up in reviews as both the best thing about UFC 6 and its biggest flaw.

EA's own June 18 update said Flow State balance is "under active investigation." That's company language for "we know the competitive crowd is unhappy, and we don't have a fix yet." A July patch is coming. Whether that means changing how the system works or just tuning the numbers underneath it, EA hasn't said.

Sixty fighters are just gone

While Flow State has been the loud argument, a quieter one has been building across the EA Forums and review roundups: more than 60 fighters who were in UFC 5 didn't make it into UFC 6.

EA hasn't put out a full list of who got cut, and that silence is part of what's bothering people. Reviewers have flagged the gap. Players are building their own comparison spreadsheets in the absence of an official one. The only thing EA has actually committed to is "monthly" roster additions after launch, with zero specific names attached to any of it.

This goes past the obvious annoyance of not finding your guy in career mode. It's a pattern that's becoming familiar across EA's sports lineup, and it's worth saying plainly: ship a smaller, tighter base roster, then sell the rest of the game back to you over the next twelve months through content drops.

There's an honest version of that approach. Live-service updates let a studio fix what's broken and add what players actually ask for, instead of locking everything in a year before launch and hoping it holds up. UFC also has real licensing churn. Fighters retire, get poached by rival promotions, or just leave the roster, and a yearly release has to absorb that somehow.

There's also a dishonest version, and it's the one people are currently mad about. A 60-fighter cut with no published list and no return dates isn't roster management. It's a content gap wearing a live-service costume. EA hasn't said which version this is. The "monthly additions" line is vague enough to be either one, and that vagueness is exactly what turns a roster complaint into a trust problem.

This isn't happening in a vacuum, either. EA's other yearly sports titles have leaned on the same playbook for years. Madden trims rosters and depth charts at launch, then patches in accuracy updates and content through the season. FIFA, now EA Sports FC, ships a base squad list and backfills real-world transfers for months afterward. The difference with UFC 6 is that fighters aren't transferring between teams on a weekly news cycle the way footballers and NFL players are. A roster gap here reads less like keeping pace with a moving sport and more like a launch decision that happened to leave 60 fighters on the cutting room floor, with the explanation arriving after the complaints did instead of before them.

So should you buy a sports game at launch in 2026?

This is the part that actually matters if you're deciding what to do this weekend.

UFC 6's career mode, the Legacy story prologue, and Hall of Legends all seem to be in solid shape right now. Multiple reviewers played through these offline modes without running into the crash problems. If that's what you're here for, the day-one build is close to what EA promised.

Online is a different animal. Ranked has been hit with white-screen crashes that hand the disconnected player a loss anyway, matchmaking failures, and the same Flow State confusion splitting the reviews. EA has already confirmed all of it. A July patch is coming, but coming isn't here, and buying primarily for ranked play this week means buying into a problem EA has already admitted exists, with no fixed date for when it stops.

None of this is unique to UFC 6, either. It's close to the default state of a sports game launch now. The yearly release cycle doesn't leave much room for a long, quiet bug-fixing window before the door opens, and the real stress test only starts once a few hundred thousand actual players hit the servers at the same time. The first patch after launch has stopped being the exception. It's basically part of the release schedule at this point.

So the honest answer is it depends on what you're buying it for. That's a less satisfying answer than yes or no, but it's the true one. Playing solo through career and story content, day one is fine. If ranked is the entire reason you're buying the game, you might want to wait for July and see what actually changes, instead of finding out the hard way that a dropped connection just cost you a match you didn't lose.

A quick checklist before you buy

If you're still deciding, this is roughly what matters right now. Buying for career mode, Legacy, or Hall of Legends: go ahead, the offline experience is reportedly stable and is where most of the praise in this review cycle is actually coming from. Buying mainly for ranked online: hold off until the July patch lands and check patch notes before committing real time to the grind, since a loss from a server crash still counts against your record today. Buying because your favorite fighter is missing from the roster and you're hoping a patch brings them back: there's no guarantee, and EA hasn't named names, so don't buy on that hope alone. Buying the Ultimate Edition for early access: the early access window has already closed, so that's no longer a reason to pay extra.

A strong Metacritic score and a rocky online launch used to feel like a contradiction. They're not anymore. Increasingly, they're just both true at the same time, in the same week, for the same game.
