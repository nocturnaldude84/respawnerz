GTA 6's Ultimate Edition Doesn't Just Include Extras, It Locks Entire Businesses Behind A Paywall

Premium editions have always come with bonus vehicles and outfits. Nobody loses sleep over that. But locking businesses and locations inside a $20 upgrade is a different conversation, and players are noticing.

June 24, 2026 by Respawnerz Staff 8 min read

Premium editions are part of the furniture now. You pay more, you get a steelbook or a bonus outfit or an in-game vehicle that handles slightly better than the starter car. Most players shrug and move on. The people who want the extras buy up, the people who don't skip it, and everyone plays the same game.

That was the reasonable assumption going into GTA 6's pre-order rollout. A bonus car, maybe. Some Vice City-themed cosmetics. Something that looks good in a screenshot but doesn't change how you experience Leonida.

The GTA 6 Ultimate Edition appears to do something different. According to what Rockstar Games has outlined for the $109.99 tier, the upgrade includes access to specific businesses and locations that Standard Edition buyers at $89.99 will not have from launch. That detail has been sitting in the fine print, and players are now reading it carefully.

The question worth asking is not whether $20 is a lot of money. The question is whether locking a piece of Vice City behind a paywall is the same category of decision as locking a jacket.

Why Cosmetic Bonuses Have Never Really Started Fights

It helps to think about why premium edition bonuses usually land without controversy. The pattern goes back years. Cyberpunk 2077 had collector's editions with lore books and a jacket. Red Dead Redemption 2's Ultimate Edition came with bank robbery missions and bonus outfits. Call of Duty games routinely bundle operator skins and weapon blueprints into higher tiers. Almost none of it caused lasting backlash.

The reason is fairly simple. A skin does not change the world. If another player has a different jacket or a shinier car, your version of the game is not missing anything functional. You explore the same map, complete the same missions, interact with the same systems. The gap is cosmetic in the truest sense.

Weapons sometimes push that line a little further, because a powerful weapon in the first hour of a game can change how you play. But most publishers balance those bonuses carefully, and players have mostly accepted them as minor advantages that fade quickly once the game opens up.

The threshold tends to hold as long as both editions are playing the same game. What Rockstar appears to be testing with GTA 6 is what happens when that stops being true.

Businesses Are Not Cosmetics

Here is where the GTA 6 Ultimate Edition gets genuinely interesting, and not in a way Rockstar probably wants discussed.

Businesses in GTA have always been more than decoration. In GTA V and GTA Online, owning a business means owning an income stream, a set of activities, a home base, and sometimes a piece of the story you build around your character. A nightclub in GTA Online is not just a building. It is how you generate passive income, how you interact with specific mission types, how your character fits into the economy of Los Santos. The business is the mechanic.

If GTA 6's Ultimate Edition gives buyers early or exclusive access to specific businesses or locations in Vice City, that is a different conversation than a bonus outfit. Players who pick up the GTA 6 Standard Edition may find themselves walking past properties they cannot access, operating in a version of Leonida with fewer options than the player next to them paid to unlock. That might not matter much in a single-player context if the businesses are purely cosmetic or purely optional. But it will matter enormously in GTA Online, where property ownership, income, and territory are central to how the game works.

It also matters for immersion in a way that cosmetics do not. A character wearing a different shirt does not break your sense of being inside a world. A business you cannot enter because you didn't pay for the right edition does exactly that. It puts a visible seam in the map, and it is the kind of seam that is hard to ignore once you've seen it.

Rockstar has not confirmed the full details of how these businesses will function, what they include, or whether they affect progression. Those are important unknowns. But the framing alone, calling these features Ultimate Edition exclusives rather than early access bonuses, is enough to raise the concern.

Rockstar Is Probably Running An Experiment

Rockstar is not a company that makes decisions by accident. GTA Online generated an extraordinary amount of revenue over more than a decade. Shark Cards, premium content packs, new businesses and properties sold to returning players, it all added up to one of the most profitable live service operations in gaming history. The company understands, better than almost anyone, how to attach monetary value to in-game property.

The move to include businesses in the GTA 6 Ultimate Edition looks like an extension of that thinking. If players have already demonstrated a willingness to spend real money on virtual property in GTA Online, it is not a stretch to assume some of that logic was applied when structuring the launch editions for GTA 6.

The $20 gap between Standard and Ultimate is also worth thinking about. Twenty dollars is not a small amount, but it is not a prohibitive one either. That price point seems intentionally designed to feel like a reasonable upgrade rather than a premium tier. The hope, presumably, is that enough players will look at the extras list, see businesses and locations alongside the vehicles and cash bonuses, and decide the gap is worth closing. If even a fraction of GTA 6's enormous launch audience makes that calculation, the revenue adds up fast.

Whether that strategy works is a separate question from whether it is the right call for players. Those two things are not always in conflict, but in this case they might be.

The Case For, and Against, the Ultimate Edition

It is worth being fair here, because this debate has more than one side.

Players who support the GTA 6 Ultimate Edition structure have reasonable points to make. Premium editions have always existed to give buyers who spend more something genuinely worthwhile in return. If the only extras were a car and an outfit, the upgrade would feel hollow. Adding businesses and locations gives it real weight. And if those businesses are available to Standard Edition players later, whether through normal progression or separate purchase, the argument that anyone is being permanently locked out becomes harder to sustain.

There is also the straightforward consumer choice argument. Nobody is forced to buy the Ultimate Edition. The GTA 6 Standard Edition is still a full game. If Rockstar delivers everything it has promised in that base package, the question of what the upgrade includes becomes secondary for most players.

But the case against is not just about the money. It is about what the decision signals. If businesses and locations can be separated from the base game at launch, through edition tiers rather than post-launch DLC, that changes how the model works. Post-launch content has always been sold separately, and players have largely accepted that. Splitting the day-one game by edition is a different thing. It means two players who both buy GTA 6 on launch day are not playing the same version of Vice City, and that asymmetry starts at the moment they open the game.

Standard Edition buyers may not feel they are getting a reduced experience immediately. But if those businesses turn out to be meaningful, even in ways that only become clear after a few dozen hours of play, the perception of what they paid for will shift.

The Questions Rockstar Still Hasn't Answered

There are several things about the GTA 6 Ultimate Edition bonus businesses that remain unclear, and those gaps matter a lot for how this ends up being judged.

The most obvious one is whether the exclusivity is permanent. If Standard Edition players can access these businesses through normal in-game progression, or purchase them separately at a later date, the controversy is smaller. If they are locked permanently to Ultimate Edition owners, that changes the conversation entirely.

It also matters whether these businesses affect GTA 6's story. Cosmetic properties, places that look interesting but don't interact with missions or progression, are a very different thing from businesses that generate income, unlock activities, or factor into the main or side content. Rockstar has not been specific enough about this distinction for anyone to say with certainty which category these fall into.

The GTA Online implications are arguably the biggest unanswered question of all. GTA 6's online component is expected to be the long tail of Rockstar's revenue strategy, and if Ultimate Edition business bonuses carry over in a meaningful way into that mode, the gap between editions becomes much harder to dismiss. A cosmetic advantage fades. An economic or territorial one in a live service game compounds over time.

There is also the question of what happens to the GTA 6 pre-order audience that already committed to the Standard Edition before these details were widely understood. Whether Rockstar provides any clarity before the November launch, or leaves players to discover the differences after launch, will affect how the community processes this.

This Isn't Really About Twenty Dollars

The debate around the GTA 6 Ultimate Edition is going to get framed as a price argument, because that is the easiest version of the story to tell. Twenty dollars more, and you get more stuff. You either think it is worth it or you don't.

But that framing misses what players are actually reacting to. The real discomfort is about category. Grand Theft Auto games have always sold themselves on freedom. You can go anywhere. You can do anything. The map is yours. That promise is so central to the GTA identity that it shapes everything from how the games are marketed to how players talk about them. Restricting access to businesses and locations based on which edition you purchased puts a condition on that freedom that feels different from selling you a different-colored jacket.

Rockstar has earned enormous goodwill with its audience, and a lot of players will give the company the benefit of the doubt until they have the game in their hands and can see exactly what the differences look like in practice. That is fair. The details we have right now are incomplete, and it is possible the Ultimate Edition businesses turn out to be minor enough that this entire conversation looks overcalibrated in November.

But if they are not minor, if these are meaningful properties that affect how players experience Vice City, then Rockstar will have tested something that other publishers are watching closely. The major AAA studios are always looking at what each other gets away with. If Rockstar can sell businesses and locations as edition-exclusive launch content without significant pushback, the practice will show up in other games within a few years. The debate happening right now around GTA 6's pre-orders is, in that sense, about more than one game.

That is worth paying attention to, even if you've already clicked buy.
